## 1️⃣ Subject–Verb Agreement

### Rule

- Singular subject → singular verb
- Plural subject → plural verb

### Example

**Studies have found…**

- Subject: *Studies* (plural)
- Verb: *have found*

If singular:

**Research has shown…**

✔ Always identify the real subject first.

---

## 2️⃣ Present Perfect in Research Context

### Rule

Use **has/have + V3** when:

- Talking about research findings
- The result is still true now

### Example

**International studies have found that…**

Another common pattern:

**Evidence has suggested that…**

---

## 3️⃣ Present Perfect Passive (Result Focus)

### Rule

Use when the result is important, not the doer:

**has/have + been + V3**

### Example

**Child poverty has been shown to be a key factor.**

Common FIB patterns:

- has been shown to be
- has been found to be

✔ If “to be” comes after the blank → passive is often correct.

---

## 4️⃣ Passive Voice (General Rule)

### Rule

**Subject + be + V3**

### Example

The report **was submitted** yesterday.

---

## 5️⃣ Conjunction Must Be Followed by Full Clause

### Rule

After:

- as
- although
- because
- while
- when

You must have:

**conjunction + subject + finite verb**

### Example

Correct:

**as mothers enter employment**

Wrong:

as mothers entering ❌

✔ “Entering” has no tense.

---

## 6️⃣ General Facts → Present Simple

### Rule

Use present simple for general truths or patterns.

### Example

Countries **have** higher employment rates.

---

## 7️⃣ Preposition Collocation Rule

### Rule

Choose the preposition that naturally fits the verb/noun.

### Example

generate revenue **through** taxation

Not:

- by revenue ❌
- within revenue ❌

✔ Grammar may look correct in all options, but only one sounds natural.

---

## 8️⃣ Dummy Subject Structure

### Rule

**There + intransitive verb + real subject**

### Example

There **comes** a moment when everything changes.

---

## 9️⃣ Relative (Adjective) Clause

### Rule

**that / who / which + verb** (describes a noun)

### Example

The book **that you gave me** is interesting.

---

## 🔟 Adverbial Clause of Time

### Rule

**when + subject + verb**

### Example

I remember the day **when we first met**.

---

## 1️⃣1️⃣ Possessive Pronoun Agreement

### Rule

Possessive pronoun must match the subject.

### Example

The company changed **its** policy.

---

## 1️⃣2️⃣ “Own” Usage

### Rule

**own** must follow a possessive determiner.

### Example

She completed **her own** project.

---

## 1️⃣3️⃣ Present Perfect After “This is the first time…”

### Rule

This is the first/second/only time + subject + **have/has + V3**

### Example

This is the first time I **have visited** Paris.

---

## 1️⃣4️⃣ Participial Phrase (Reason / Condition)

### Rule

V3/adjective phrase + comma + main clause

### Example

**Exhausted after the race**, he went home.

---

## 1️⃣5️⃣ Compound Verb (One Subject, Two Actions)

### Rule

One subject + verb1 + and + verb2

### Example

She **opened** the door and **entered** the room.

---

## 1️⃣6️⃣ Verb Pattern: Help

### Rule

help + base verb

help + to + base verb

### Example

This guide helps students **understand** grammar.

---

## 1️⃣7️⃣ Verb Pattern: Enable

### Rule

enable + object + to + base verb

### Example

The scholarship enabled her **to study** abroad.

---

## 1️⃣8️⃣ Reporting Verb Structure

### Rule

Subject + reporting verb + (that) clause

### Example

The teacher said **that the test was difficult**.

---

## 1️⃣9️⃣ Noun Clause After “That”

### Rule

Verb + that + subject + verb

### Example

She believes **that hard work leads to success**.

---

## 2️⃣0️⃣ “One of the + Superlative + Plural Noun + To-Infinitive”

### Rule

one of the first/last/next + plural noun + **to + base verb**

### Example

He was one of the first students **to arrive**.

---

## 2️⃣1️⃣ Infinitive as Post-Modifier

### Rule

Noun + **to + base verb**

### Example

She has a lot of work **to complete**.

---

## 2️⃣4️⃣ Adverb Modifies Adjective

### Rule

Use an **adverb** to describe an adjective.

Structure: adverb + adjective

### Example

A specimen must have a **distinctly green** color.

---

## 2️⃣5️⃣ Reason Connector (Cause Clause)

### Rule

Use **since / because + subject + verb** to show reason (not condition).

### Example

**Since** the beryl’s color is greenish blue, it is classified as aquamarine.

---

## 2️⃣6️⃣ Fixed Collocation: “A Source of”

### Rule

a source of + noun

### Example

This color definition is a source of **confusion**.

---

## 2️⃣7️⃣ Passive Form of “Draw the Line”

### Rule

draw the line = set a boundary

Passive structure: be + past participle

### Example

Professionals disagree on where the dividing lines should be **drawn**.

---

## 2️⃣8️⃣ Collocation: “Have an Impact on/Upon”

### Rule

have + impact + on/upon + noun

### Example

Calling a gem an emerald can have a significant **impact upon** its price.

## 2️⃣9️⃣ Adverbs of Degree / Intensity

**Rule:** Use an **adverb** to modify an adjective to indicate intensity, degree, or emphasis.

**Example:** This type of pollution is **exceptionally high** in coastal regions.

*Explanation:* “Exceptionally” modifies “high” to show that the pollution level is unusually strong.

---

## 3️⃣0️⃣ Idiomatic / Collocation Phrase

**Rule:** Use fixed phrases or collocations when expressing partial influence or manner.

**Example:** Many species’ behavior depends, **in some way**, on natural cycles of light and dark.

*Explanation:* “in some way” is the correct idiomatic expression showing partial reliance; singular form is used for formal scientific writing.

---

## 3️⃣1️⃣ Present Participle for Method / Ongoing Action

**Rule:** Use a **present participle (V-ing)** to indicate a method, manner, or continuous action connected to the main clause.

**Example:** Turtles show orientation to the water, **relying mostly** on light cues.

*Explanation:* “relying” connects the turtles’ method of orienting themselves to the main action.

---

## 3️⃣2️⃣ Cause + Object + Infinitive

**Rule:** After verbs like **cause, enable, allow**, use **object + to-infinitive** structure.

**Example:** Artificial lighting on beaches can cause **them to move** away from the sea.

*Explanation:* The structure clearly shows the cause-effect relationship: lighting (cause) → turtles (object) → move (infinitive action).
