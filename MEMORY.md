## Why 92% vs 76%: What We Learned (Apeuni Validated 2026-03-10)

### 92% version (current essay) – what it does right

1. **Paragraph structure matches .cursorrules exactly**
   - P1: 4 sentences (general, first side, other side, thesis).
   - P2: 5 sentences (intro, point, explain, one example, Therefore). Not 6 or 7.
   - P3: 4 sentences (intro, point, explain, one example). Not 5 or 6.
   - P4: 2 sentences (overall conclusion, stance). Not 3.
   When we broke these counts (e.g. 6 sentences in P2 or P3, or 3 in P4), the score dropped to 76%.

2. **P1 – Simple intro and prompt echo**
   - First sentence: "In our technological world, new inventions appear every single day." No "Considering that" or "In view of" (those caused 60%/76%).
   - Fourth sentence: "This essay will discuss the advantages and disadvantages of the smartphone and determine whether its impact is beneficial or detrimental, using a pragmatic approach."
   - Echo the prompt: use "determine" and "beneficial or detrimental" (prompt wording). Do NOT add "and argue that advantages outweigh" in P1; save the conclusion for P4.

3. **P2 – One example sentence, no extra weighing**
   - Invention: "The smartphone combines a phone, the internet, and applications in one tool."
   - Example and result in one sentence: "For example, during the COVID-19 pandemic in 2020, schools closed, but smartphones allowed children without computers to continue learning, and as a result millions kept learning."
   - End with: "Therefore, the smartphone has a positive impact in these areas." Nothing after that. No "and in many ways remains valuable for society" or similar (redundant weighing caused 76%).

4. **P3 – Four sentences, rebuttal inside the fourth**
   - Fourth sentence = one example + outcome + rebuttal: "For example, at family dinners people look at their phones instead of talking to each other, which weakens real connections; as a result, some experience worse well-being, but these drawbacks stem from use, not the device, and when people set limits, they can avoid them."
   - Must include: "For example", "their phones" (not "phones"), "as a result, some experience worse well-being", and the exact rebuttal "they stem from use, not the device, and when people set limits, they can avoid them." Do NOT split into extra sentences (e.g. "Although these drawbacks are real" as its own sentence) or drop "As a result, some experience worse well-being"; the logical chain broke and score dropped.

5. **P4 – Two sentences, simple stance**
   - First: "In conclusion, the smartphone brings benefits in communication, education, and emergency access, but has also raised concerns about screen time and well-being." ("in daily life" is optional; 92% achieved without it.)
   - Second: "My conviction is that the case for benefit is stronger than the case for harm, and with sensible use it remains valuable." (NO comma before "it".)
   - Do NOT add: "we can determine", "the impact depends on how we use it", "To sum up", or "it is clearly beneficial when used in balance" as extra phrasing. Simple, direct conclusion = 92%.

## Rules for 92%+ Argument Quality (Any Essay)

### 1. Structure first (from .cursorrules)
- Follow the 4-paragraph structure strictly: sentence roles and sentence counts per paragraph.
- P1: 4 sentences. P2: 4–5 sentences. P3: 4 sentences. P4: 1–2 sentences.
- Do not add or split sentences to "improve" logic if it breaks these counts.

### 2. Content and wording
- **Describe the invention:** If the prompt asks to describe something, define it clearly (e.g. "combines a phone, the internet, and applications in one tool").
- **Echo the prompt:** Use the prompt’s key verbs and phrases (e.g. "determine", "beneficial or detrimental") in the thesis (P1). Do not pre-state your conclusion in P1.
- **PEEL in P2 and P3:** Point, Explain, Example, Link. Use "For example" before examples and "As a result" before outcomes; keep "Therefore" before the link in P2. Example and result can be in one sentence when structure limits sentence count.
- **Specific evidence:** Named examples (e.g. COVID-19 pandemic in 2020), numbers (e.g. millions), and clear groups (e.g. children without computers).
- **Rebuttal (when relevant):** Use the causal line: harms "stem from use, not the device, and when people set limits, they can avoid them." Do not add a second weighing sentence.
- **Conclusion:** "My conviction is that the case for benefit is stronger than the case for harm" (or equivalent). Keep the conclusion to 1–2 sentences; no extra "we can determine" or "depends on how we use it".

### 3. Avoid (these lowered the score)
- Do not put the conclusion in the intro (e.g. "argue that advantages outweigh" in P1).
- Do not add a second conclusion or weighing phrase at the end of P2.
- Do not add "we can determine" or "the impact depends on how we use it" in P4.
- Do not use "Considering that", "In view of", or "Furthermore" as linkers (simpler openers and flow scored higher).
- Do not add UNESCO or similar citations if one strong example is enough.
- Do not change "My conviction" to "My determination", or "case for harm" to "case for detriment", or "their phones" to "phones" in the example.
- Do not remove "For example" before an example or "As a result" before an outcome when the logical chain needs it.

### 4. Grammar and style (from past feedback)
- Use "instead of talking to each other" (not "rather than talking").
- Use "has a positive impact" (not "positively impacts").
- NO comma before "it" in: "with sensible use it remains valuable." (Adding a comma caused 76%.)
- High-school level English; avoid stuff, things, really. Do not over-vary vocabulary at the cost of argument clarity.

## Why 76% went to 92%: Word count and full phrasing (Apeuni 2026-03-10)

The smartphone essay scored **76%** when we trimmed it to 250–265 words (removing "using a pragmatic approach", "reliable", "when they are in danger", "it is among the most influential", "in daily life", "has also raised"). It scored **92%** when we restored those phrases and kept the essay in the **270–275 word** range.

**Takeaway:** Do not cut MEMORY-validated phrasing just to save words. Apeuni rewards the full thesis, full P2 support, and full P4 conclusion. **Word count target:** 270–275.

This essay will discuss the advantages and disadvantages of the smartphone and determine whether its impact is beneficial or detrimental, using a pragmatic approach."

P2: "The smartphone combines a phone, the internet, and applications in one tool. It has changed how we communicate, work, and access information, and is among the most influential. It benefits society because it gives people reliable access to vital services and information; people can call for help when in danger and students can learn when schools are closed. For example, during the COVID-19 pandemic in 2020, schools closed, but smartphones allowed children without computers to continue learning, and as a result millions kept learning. Therefore, the smartphone has a positive impact in these areas."

P3: "On the other hand, some argue that smartphones damage health and social life. Excessive use is linked to sleep problems and less activity, and social media can increase stress. When people use phones too much, they spend less time face-to-face. For example, at family dinners people look at their phones instead of talking to each other, which weakens real connections; as a result, some experience worse well-being, but these drawbacks stem from use, not the device, and when people set limits, they can avoid them."

P4: "In conclusion, the smartphone brings benefits in communication, education, and emergency access, but has also raised concerns about screen time and well-being. My conviction is that the case for benefit is stronger than the case for harm, and with sensible use it remains valuable."
